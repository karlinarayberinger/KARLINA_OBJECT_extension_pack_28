The most irreducibly small particles, as understood by current physics, are the **elementary particles**. These are particles that cannot be divided into smaller components. They are the fundamental building blocks of matter and the universe according to the **Standard Model of particle physics**.

### Examples of Elementary Particles
1. **Quarks**:
   - Six types ("flavors"): up, down, charm, strange, top, bottom.
   - Combine to form protons and neutrons.

2. **Leptons**:
   - Includes the electron, muon, tau, and their associated neutrinos (electron neutrino, muon neutrino, tau neutrino).

3. **Gauge Bosons**:
   - Particles that mediate the fundamental forces of nature:
     - Photon (electromagnetic force).
     - Gluons (strong force).
     - W and Z bosons (weak force).

4. **Higgs Boson**:
   - Gives particles mass through the Higgs field.

### Are They Truly Irreducible?
- As far as we know, these particles are point-like, meaning they have no known substructure and no measurable size. However, it is theoretically possible that they could be composed of even smaller entities, which might be discovered through future advancements in physics.

### Beyond the Standard Model
- Theories like **string theory** propose that these particles might not be point-like but are instead tiny vibrating "strings" of energy. If this is true, then strings would be the most fundamental entities.

For now, elementary particles as described by the Standard Model are considered the most irreducibly small.

### How Are Elementary Particles Measured?

Elementary particles are not "measured" in the same way as macroscopic objects because they are incredibly small, often exhibiting behavior governed by quantum mechanics. Instead, their properties (mass, charge, spin, etc.) are inferred using advanced experiments and mathematical models. Here's how:

---

#### 1. **Particle Accelerators**
   - Devices like the **Large Hadron Collider (LHC)** accelerate particles to near-light speeds and smash them together.
   - By analyzing the collision debris, scientists identify new particles and measure their properties (e.g., mass, charge, and lifetime).
   - Example: The discovery of the Higgs boson in 2012 relied on collisions creating particles that decayed in ways predicted by the Standard Model.

---

#### 2. **Detectors**
   - Huge detectors surrounding collision points record traces of particles.
   - Particles leave measurable effects such as ionization, Cherenkov radiation, or electromagnetic signals that reveal their properties indirectly.
   - Example: Electrons are identified by the curved paths they take in magnetic fields.

---

#### 3. **Scattering Experiments**
   - The technique of bombarding particles (like electrons or neutrons) onto targets allows scientists to observe how particles scatter.
   - The patterns of scattering provide clues about the target's structure and the forces at play.
   - Example: Rutherford's gold foil experiment revealed the atomic nucleus.

---

#### 4. **Indirect Observation via Quantum Effects**
   - Elementary particles' effects on their surroundings are measured to infer their existence.
   - Example: Neutrinos rarely interact with matter, but massive detectors (like Super-Kamiokande) observe rare interactions with water molecules.

---

### What Does "No Measurable Size" Mean?

When we say elementary particles have "no measurable size," we mean they are **point-like** in current physics models. This has specific implications:

---

#### 1. **Point-Like Nature**
   - In the Standard Model, elementary particles are considered zero-dimensional points.
   - They lack internal structure, meaning they aren't composed of smaller parts.

---

#### 2. **No Spatial Extension**
   - Unlike protons or atoms, which have measurable diameters due to their composite nature, elementary particles have no "radius."
   - Experiments probing particles at incredibly small scales (e.g., using high-energy collisions) have found no evidence of a finite size.

---

#### 3. **Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle**
   - Quantum mechanics adds complexity: particles exhibit wave-particle duality, meaning their "position" isn't sharply defined.
   - At extremely small scales, uncertainty dominates, making the notion of a definite "size" less meaningful.

---

### Experimental Limits on Size

Physicists place upper bounds on particle sizes based on experimental data. For example:
- The **electron** has an effective radius smaller than \(10^{-18}\) meters, as determined by scattering experiments.
- Below this scale, no deviations from "point-like" behavior have been observed.

### Why No Measurable Size?

This concept stems from:
- **Mathematical Modeling**: The equations describing particles (e.g., in quantum field theory) treat them as mathematical points.
- **Empirical Observations**: Experiments so far have not revealed any substructure.

### Open Questions
- Some theories, like **string theory**, propose that particles have a tiny finite size as vibrating strings (\(10^{-35}\) meters, the Planck length).
- Future experiments with higher energy levels may reveal whether particles are truly point-like or have an internal structure.
